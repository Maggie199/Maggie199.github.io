Redefine Online Purchase Experience of Bio-Rad T100™ Thermal Cycler

UX Designer

Project

T100™ Thermal Cycler Webpage Redesign

Bio-Rad Laboratories

Time

UX Designer

Tools

Background

As Bio-Rad gets more products in its portfolio, it seeks to sell more commoditized products via web, so that sales representatives could focus on higher value products.

Objective

Redefine Online Purchase Experience of Bio-Rad T100™ Thermal Cycler

Major Steps

  • - 10 interviews with internal stakeholders
  • - 12 competitive usability evaluations
  • - 8 interviews with customers
  • - 38 online survey questions
  • - 4 usability testings

Deliverables

  • - Customer journey map
  • - Behavior variables
  • - Primary, secondary and supplementary personas
  • - Low-fi prototype
  • - Hi-fi prototype

troubleshooting

Success Business

Bio-Rad Laboratories(NYSE: BIO and BIOb), ranks in the world's top five life science companies and maintains a solid reputation for quality and innovation.

Growing products portfolio

As Bio-Rad gets more products in its portfolio, it seeks to sell more commoditized products via web, so that sales representatives could focus on higher value products.

10+ year old website

The bio-rad.com was launched back in 1997, and has lots of room for improvement to be a trendy website: hero images, card layouts, responsive design, etc...

Low Findability and Discoverability

Bio-rad.com uses a Deep hierarchy info architecture; product detail pages are 6 levels down from the homepage, and not reflecting attribute priorities in the Mini-IA.

1

Who is using our website? How're our competitors doing?

Conducted interviews on a wide range of internal stakeholders, including product managers, sales representatives, technical support and customer service, to learn about the most typical scenario when customers decide they need a thermal cycler, what information they need to make a purchase decision, how they interact with bio-rad to get those info, and most importantly what kind of group is likely to be our online purchasing target users.

Researched on the websites of 6 top competitors in life science field and 6 non-peer vendors to compare their features and content, as well as branstorm the best online purchasing experience.

  • 10 interviews on internal stakeholders
  • 12 competitive usability evaluations
2

What steps do customers go through in engaging with our website?

Put together the data collected in step 1 in spreadsheets and performed data interpretation and consolidation using customer journey map, which illustrates the steps that customers go through in engaging with our online purchasing system.

  • Original customer journey map
  • Revised customer journey map
3

What do customers say?

Narrowed down target user group based on the result of step2, and conducted interviews on existing customers from UC Berkerley, UC Davis and the Buck Institute, to learn about the most important factors that influence their purchase decisions and their opinion about the current T100 webpage.

Designed an online survey using Survey Monkey upon accomplishment of the Questionnaire Design for Social Surveys course on Coursera, to further collect the above info from a larger user group. The survey includes 38 questions in total, and up to 13 questions for a single user deponding on his role(lab manager, post doc, researcher and purchaser). The number of open-ended question is limited to 1 per user so it could be finished in ~5 mins. This Flow Chart describes the page logic amongst questions.

  • 8 interviews on customers
  • 38 online survey questions
4

What does the interview result tell us?

Interpreted the information collected in step 3 with 30 Behavior Variables grouped in 3 sets: background, decision process and web-based needs, with Adobe Illustrator. The goal is to identify clusters of participants developing at different points along the sliders.

  • 30 Behavior Variables
5

What do our typical users look like, and what they want to achieve?

Analyzed the grouping of the behaviours across on the sliders in step4, and found trends where the same participants are grouped together across multiple variables. Used Adobe Illustrator to create 3 prioritized personas and scenarioes describing how they would interact with our system, also identified their end goals, life goals and experience goals.

  • Primary, Secondary and Supplementary Personas
6

How did I prioritize and meet personas' needs? Did it work?

Designed the low-fi prototype using Balsamiq based on the goals of personas and the priority of them. According to the scenario we created, personas rarely use mobile devices to purchase/make purchase decision since it's a big deal in terms of money, so that responsive design was not included.

Improved the prototype iteratively by doing Usability Testing with internal stakeholders.

  • Low-fi Prototype
  • 4 usability testings
7

The End Result

Built a high fidelity prototype with Axure based on the mockup created in step6, iteratively improved it based on feedback from internal stakeholders, and a high fidelity interactive demo is available here. Also explored and designed different checkout methods to improve bundle sales during the refine provess.

  • Hi-fi Prototype

UX Designer

Project

Client

Cornell Dining

2013.08 - 2013.12

Hammad Malik, Maggie Nan, Ying Zhou, Lin Li

UX Designer

Background

The 745 acre Cornell campus hosts more than 30 eateries, whose operating time and menus vary and subjected to changes, causing inconvenience for students to choose eateries and food.

Objective

Help students to narrow down their eatery selections based on their cuisine preferences, health goals, expected price ranges and location, especially for those who are new.

Major Steps

  • - 8 initial interviews
  • - 20 revised interviews
  • - 13 response from online survey
  • - Quantitative analysis
  • - Conceptual Design
  • - 5 Storyboards
  • - 6 usability testings

Deliverables

  • - Initial and revised flow models

troubleshooting

Numerous dining selections

The 745 acre Cornell campus hosts more than 30 eateries, which include All You Care To Eat dining rooms, cafes, coffee houses, and food courts, and convenience stores.

Unavoidable changes

The operating time and menus of different eateries are varied and subjected to changes, causing inconvenience for students to choose dining places and food.

Meet individual needs

With so many options, students need to narrow it down based on their cuisine preferences, health goals, expected price ranges and more, especially for those who are new.

Localized features

When life gets busy, finding out the closest eatery quickly and learning about the expected walking distance & waiting time become extremely important.

1

What can we help with our target users?

Conducted interviews on 8 participants to learn about their daily activities and how they make food decisions. For the purpose of our study, any full time Cornell student was considered an eligible inquiry participant and a potential app user.

Analyzed the responses and recognized common patterns that lead to food decisions, including but not limited to: close to location, price, taste, waiting time and availability, etc...

What do users expect for from our system?

Refined interview questions based on the result in step 1 and conducted a second round interview to understand how important students consider one factor over another and their expectations on our app. Performed quantitative analysis on the interview results.

Designed an online survey to increase our sample size and learn about how do factors like class and campus location affect students' preferences.

  • 20 revised interviews
  • 13 response from online survey
  • quantitative analysis
3

What would be involved in our system? What's their relationship?

Created Flow Model based on the result of step2 to identify work roles inside our system’s work domain and communications among different work roles and system components.

Constructed the Work Activity Affinity Diagram(WAAD) to consolidate and interpret work activity data, which was divided into 3 broad groups: Dining Place Information, Food Information and Special Features.

  • initial and revised flow models
  • work activity affinity diagram
4

What tasks do our users want to complete? How do we organize them?

Built the social model of the system with key Work Roles and User Classes defined based on step3, to show the possible social interaction/influences among key work roles in the system.

Created Hierarchical Task Inventory (THI) by sorting system tasks into categories and breaking big tasks into sub-tasks, to provide an understanding of the tasks users need to perform to achieve certain goals.

  • Social Model
  • Hierarchical Task Inventory
5

How do our typical users look like?

Identified common characteristics of our targeted user group based on the models defined in step4 and conducted persona interviews on qualified candidates to validate and learn more about their goals and requirements.

Designed personas according to the interview results, and prioritized them using the official Cornell demographic stats on student population.

  • 6 Persona Interviews
  • 4 Prioritized Personas
6

What are the needs of our personas?

Brainstormed the functionality of our system based on the goals of personas and their priorities, then evaluated different ideas via sketching and critiquing.

  • Multiple Conceptual Design Ideas
7

How would personas interact with our system?

Evaluated the alternative conceptual design ideas generated in step6 via storytelling and got the final conceptual design .

  • 5 Story Boards
  • Final Concept Design Sketch

GitHub commit messages tell us a lot about software dev project progress, which is important for project managers to guarantee the overall team efficiency.

Objectives

  • - Track developer's coding performance by parsing commit messages
  • - Break project into parts for developers to track their own progress
  • - Show the progress of whole project to PMs by data visualization
  • - Calculates difference between expected & actual performance

Major Steps

troubleshooting

performance gap

People make plans everyday, but the gap between expected and the actual performance is rarely closed.

GitHub msg vs project progress

GitHub commit messages tell us a lot about software dev project progress, which is important for project managers to guarantee the overall team efficiency

The Challenges

Tracks developer's coding performance by parsing their commit messages
Breaks project into milestones/sections for developers to track their own progress
Shows the progress of whole project to PMs by data visualization
Calculates difference between expected & actual performance on a daily base

What're the requirements of our client?

Demonstrated that the proposed system is technically feasible, recommended a process based on iterative refinement, and provided an outline plan.

  • - Extensibility

Designed the System Architecture of Model-­View-­Controller, to meet the requirements defined in step2. As shown below, the controller section receives user input events and communicate with Github for data collection; the view section interprets data and present it to users; while the model section writes/reads data from database and connects the other two sections.

automated emergency reporting system in car accidents

UX Designer

Project

SARA: automated emergency communication system

Time

2014.03 - 2014.05

Location

Ithaca, New York

Team

Theodore Lee, David Choi, Maggie Nan, Jaeil Lee

My Role

UX Designer

Tools

  • - Axure
  • - Adobe Illustrator

Background

Motor vehicle accidents claim unnecessary lives. When all victims are unconscious, it takes time for bystanders to find them and call emergency services.

Objective

Design an automated emergency signal system that will sent out voice-based message from cars after accidents happen, and reduce false positives.

Major Steps

  • - Ideation
  • - Competitive Evaluation
  • - 2 iterative Design
  • - Contextual interviews with 11 drivers
  • - Observations on two 911 operators
  • - Qualitative and quantitative analysis

Deliverables

  • - Use flow chart
  • - Design of 'cancel' button color, type, location and voice message

troubleshooting

emergency signal Promptness

Motor vehicle accidents claim unnecessary lives. When all victims are unconscious, it takes time for bystanders to find them and call emergency services.

information severity and accuracy

After accidents occur, it's hard for shocked victims to report necessary info in the shortest time, while bystanders cannot provide relevant medical details.

Design Challenges

Automated emergency signal from cars
Works when driver is unconscious
Voice-based message
Reduce the potential for false positives
1

How did we position our product based on existing data?

Conducted research on reported car accidents over the past two decades and ways to reduce the number of vehicular fatalities. Also evaluated the positioning of SARA in comparison to other emergency systems.

Designed the workflow of the system and identified design objectives:

  • - voice message with exact text that quantifies a legitimate 911 phone call
  • - a “cancel” button to terminate SARA’s automated response and reduce the false positives that inherent with any automated response system
  • Flow Chart
2

What was our Initial design?

Came up with an initial design for the cancel button based on features of already existent emergency services and our experiences of driving a motor vehicle.

Initial Design specifications:

  • - Medicine cap, 1-1.5” in diameter
  • - Located underneath the lip of the seat, just below where the horizontal seat adjuster would be
  • - Glowing yellow
  • - Activate by Push-turn
3

How did we evaluate our design with field research?

Conducted contextual interviews on 11 drivers, varied by age, gender, driving experience, types of cars, and number of accidents, in order to solicit opinions and obtain feedback on the initial design.

Performed Observations on two 911 operators for approximately half an hour and then conducted an interview, inquiring about their experiences as an operator, their reactions to calls received, and their criteria for determining the legitimacy of calls. Also described our initial design and solicited their comments, feedback and concerns.

  • contextual interviews with 12 drivers
  • contextual interviews with two 911 operators
  • quantitative analysis
4

What did we get from the research?

Conducted qualitative and quantitative analysis on the result from step3 and summarized the following key insights:

  • - Drivers like the idea of automated distress system
  • - Drivers' height prevents a button being placed on the floor
  • - Red is associated with emergencies and is preferred for our system
  • - Drivers prefer a simple push instead of Push-turn
  • - A button that somehow tests cognitive ability is suggested
  • - The three most popular locations were: left-hand side, mirror, and dashboard
  • - Feedback that the button press has been successful is important
  • - Primary info for 911 operators are: type of emergency, where, and a call-back number
  • - Secondary info for 911 operators: number of people, severity of injuries, etc.
  • - 911 operators reported that a male computerized voice is best
  • - 911 operators suggested a delay of 10 seconds between incident and SARA call to reduce false positives
5

the Result

Revised our initial design based on the findings listed in step4. The button will remain unlit during normal operation. Should an accident occur, it will glow red, and SARA will ask the driver whether or not they wish to cancel a 911 call. If the driver wishes to, they will press the cancel button once. Afterwards, SARA will prompt them to enter a preset code into their radio dashboard. Upon successful cancellation, SARA will report that the call has been cancelled. However, if this is not successfully completed within 10 seconds, SARA will call 911 with the text, “This is SARA reporting a vehicle accident at [location], call-back may be possible at [driver’s number]. [Additional information as available, including GPS coordinates]…”.

Composed a paper in CHI Extended Abstracts Format to summerize findings from this project.

Background

Yumso.com is a home-cooking food sharing platform. It was initially created by two back-end developers, and I joined the team to solve the severe usability issue of it.

Objective

Make the chef portal intuitive and easy to use after user's first login.

Major Steps

  • - Heuristic Evaluation
  • - 3 iterative Design
  • - Implementation

Deliverables

  • - Prototypes
  • - Live product

Design Challenges

Poorly organized contents
Lots of information required to be onboarded
Keep the onboarding process as short as possible
Best timing to collect information and the trade-offs
1

How was the system look like?

Yumso was started by two backend developers, while they achieved great functionalities, the severe usability issue caught their eyes when they are considering about release the product to real users.

In their old scenaria, when a chef user first logs in he/she will see the following screen, where you could see lots of information are "required" but there's no clue how to edit them. By further exploring of the system, user would find that he/she would not be "verified" to be onboarded to Yumso until filling out all those (and even more) info.

2

How did I evaluate it?

Came up with a list of ideas by using Jakob Nielsen's 10 general principless for heuristic evaluation. After discussion with our team of designers, engineers and marketers, we agreed on using a group of onboarding forms once the user's email address has been verified, to make sure all the required information are collected before a chef user reaches the dashboard page.

3

What is the refreshed experience?

Divided all the required information into three groups: personal info, shop info, and payment info, and created wireframes using Sketch to collect those data step by step. Iterated my design by collecting feedbacks from the rest of the team. There was a debate around when should the chef fill out the description of his/her first dish, and we decided to make it the first step after the chef is onboarded, to make the onboarding process concise and not overwhelming.

Worked with our UI designer and turned the wireframe into a high fidelity prototype, and implemented it using HTML, CSS and JavaScript with AngularJS framework with our front-end developer.

So what's happening right now?

To further improve the UX of chefs' portal, we're now coming up with a new design for chef dashboard page. In addition to the page layout, we are also reinventing the information architecture and dish uploading process. With the help from our marketing team, we're planning to run a couple rounds of usability testings with our potential users before writing a single line of code for it. A brand new yumso.com chef portal is on its way to you!
